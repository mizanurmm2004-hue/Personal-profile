Entry 01 — About Me

About Me

My name is Md. Mizanur Rahman. I'm an emerging, self-taught programmer. In this age of AI, many people believe the window for becoming a software engineer is closing — that AI will simply take the jobs. I believe the opposite: right now is one of the best times to become a software engineer. Work that once needed a team of five or six people can now be done by one person, and projects that once took months can now be built in a fraction of that time with AI's help.

To me, AI is like a large wave in the ocean — swim against it and you'll be pulled under, but ride with it and it will carry you much further than you could go alone. I believe AI has closed much of the distance between an idea and its execution. To prove that to myself, I've used my basic coding skills to find real problems around me and build working solutions for them.

Going forward, I want to go deeper into Machine Learning and Computer Vision, and bring along the people who are struggling to keep pace with technology.

Entry 03 — My Philosophy

My Theory & My Apps

There has never been a better time to be a software engineer. I know a lot of you are scared because of this whole AI wave, but if you're like me, you got into coding because you loved the idea of building something real — something another person could actually use, something that might blow up and change lives… all from just a laptop and an idea.

And honestly, that dream has never been more realistic than right now. What used to need a whole team of five people can now be done by one. Things that took years of grinding syntax, reading docs, and writing every single line by hand — you can now do in a fraction of the time.

I know everywhere you look, people in the comments are saying "AI is going to wipe out all developers." But the ones getting wiped out aren't bad coders. They're just stuck in the old way of doing things, and that world is slowly disappearing.

The people who are winning right now are different. You know that guy in your class who had five internships and a job already lined up? Or the one who built something so good that a company just hired him? Or the regular backend engineer who started using AI and suddenly owns entire systems he never touched before? Or the random dev making $50 a month from a tool he built over a weekend? Or the founders building with just two people instead of twenty?

They're all winning for the same reason — they understood the new rules faster. They figured out what to build, how to build it fast, and how to use every tool available to close the gap between their ideas and reality.

So instead of getting crushed by this wave, imagine two kinds of people:

First, the spectator. For whatever reason, they never really jumped into AI. Maybe it feels too overwhelming — LLMs, RAG, agents, GPT, Claude, OpenAI, Anthropic… after a while it all just becomes noise. Or they think using AI makes them a worse programmer, so they stay away.

Then there's the surfer. The surfer gets that programming has always been about abstraction. Nobody calls you fake for using frameworks, libraries, Stack Overflow, or copying open-source code you don't fully understand. So instead of fighting the wave, they ride it.

They use AI to learn faster, experiment faster, and close the gap between what they can imagine and what they can actually ship. Maybe you're building web apps and you scaffold an entire dashboard in a day. Or you're working on a game and AI helps you understand some hard graphics concept that would've taken hours to figure out from docs.

The leverage looks different for everyone, but the surfers are always experimenting. They shorten the distance between idea and execution. They automate the boring parts, understand new codebases quickly, prototype fast, debug faster, and learn at crazy speed. Their feedback loops are super short.

And that's becoming super important — because software engineering is turning into a creativity game. The winners are the ones who notice things, connect ideas, build weird little tools nobody thought of, and chase their curiosity before the excitement fades. A few years ago, testing a big idea had huge barriers. Now a random student can lock in for a weekend and build something thousands of people end up using.

A lot of developers still haven't accepted how big this shift really is. They're still waiting to "know enough" or master one more framework. Meanwhile, the surfers are already in the water — building, testing, learning, shipping, and adjusting.

Programming isn't about manually typing every brick anymore. It's about deciding what should exist in the first place. The bottleneck now isn't "Can you write the code?" It's "Can you think clearly? Can you spot good ideas? Can you move fast and learn quickly enough to match your own ambition?"

AI doesn't remove the need for builders — it actually raises the ceiling for those who keep building.

But here's the thing — you can't just build in silence anymore. The people getting opportunities right now (internships, referrals, startup jobs, research positions) are usually the ones who are visible. They're posting their projects on LinkedIn, making short demo videos on Twitter, or just writing about what they learned — even if it's a bug, a failed project, or a random opinion.

You don't need a perfect portfolio. You just need a visible one. Companies like OpenAI or Anthropic aren't just looking for people who are "interested" — they want builders who are already in motion.

And finally, stop overthinking whether your idea is "good enough" for your resume. Stop waiting until you feel skilled enough. The people improving fastest are the ones who just use everything available — AI, templates, GitHub repos — and build anyway. Even if it feels messy, too ambitious, or not good enough. That's exactly how you grow.

The fastest way to become that kind of programmer is to stop negotiating with yourself every time you get an idea. Just build it, ship it, show it, and iterate. Slowly your confidence and identity will catch up with your actions.

And the one thing that ties all of this together is never stopping learning.

Entry 04 — Projects

Apps I've Built

Khamari-Bondu (Farmer's Friend)

A live livestock-tracking app that helps rural farmers work out a fair market price for their animals, sends reminders for vaccinations and other essential care, and tracks an animal's growth over time through photos.

Essay Call App

An app built for elderly people with little or no formal education. Contacts are saved using voice labels and photos instead of text, so it can be used without needing to read. Family members can remotely save numbers to the elderly person's phone, and an emergency button is included for urgent situations.

View live app →

Salat Habit Making

An app designed to help build a consistent prayer habit and break free of phone addiction. Starting 10–20 minutes before prayer time it shows hadiths about the importance of prayer, auto-locks the phone at the set time, can request a photo as proof of prayer, and keeps a running count of missed prayers.
